Plastics

Spec §11 — Polymers

Where does plastic come from?

Most plastics are made from oil, a finite resource created millions of years ago from compressed sea creatures and plants. Because oil takes millions of years to form, plastic is not sustainable and will eventually run out.

Many plastics are recyclable, but a large proportion end up in landfill, where they will not biodegrade. These are the two key sustainability issues examiners expect you to know.

Not sustainable — made from finite oil Not biodegradable — stays in landfill

Key Terms

Plasticity
The ability to change shape without cracking or breaking.
Durability
The ability to withstand wear, weathering and deterioration over time.
Thermoplastic
Can be melted and reshaped repeatedly. Much easier to recycle.
Thermosetting
Once heated and cooled, holds its form permanently. Difficult to recycle.
Biopolymer
A plastic made from biological or plant materials rather than oil. PLA is the key example.
Biodegradable
Will break down naturally if buried in the ground. PLA is biodegradable; most plastics are not.

Thermoplastics Spec

THERMOPLASTIC

ABS

Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene

  • High impact resistance — tougher than HIPS
  • Makes high-quality injection mouldings with a glossy finish
  • Resistant to many chemicals
  • Takes colours well and retains them
  • More expensive than HIPS
Examples
Lego bricksSafety helmetsComputer casingsPhone cases
THERMOPLASTIC

PET

Polyethylene Terephthalate

  • One of the most widely recycled plastics
  • Excellent water and moisture barrier; also a good gas barrier
  • Easily blow moulded into bottles
  • Strong and lightweight
Examples
Water bottlesDrinks bottlesFood packaging
THERMOPLASTIC

Acrylic (PMMA)

Polymethyl Methacrylate

  • Good impact strength; lightweight
  • Can be polished to a high finish
  • Available in a wide range of colours
  • Very commonly used in school workshops
Examples
Display casesLight fittingsBaths / Signs
THERMOPLASTIC

Polythene (HDPE / LDPE)

  • Tough, resistant to chemicals
  • Flexible — can be squeezed (LDPE)
  • Good electrical insulator
  • Recyclable but large proportion ends up in landfill
Examples
Shampoo bottlesPlastic bagsMilk bottles
THERMOPLASTIC

PVC

Polyvinyl Chloride

  • Very good chemical resistance
  • Durable, tough and lightweight
  • Recyclable
Examples
Window framesPipesGuttering
THERMOPLASTIC

HIPS

High Impact Polystyrene

  • Tough; high impact strength (but lower than ABS)
  • Easily injection moulded
  • Less expensive than ABS; lower quality finish
Examples
Cheaper toysPackagingPrinter parts

Thermosetting Plastics Spec

THERMOSETTING

Urea Formaldehyde (UF)

  • Difficult to recycle
  • Once heated and cooled, cannot be reformed
  • Brittle — will crack rather than bend
  • Scratch and stain resistant
  • Gives off toxic fumes if overheated
  • Stays rigid when heated — safe for electrical use
Examples
Plug casingsLight fittingsMDF adhesive binder
THERMOSETTING

Melamine Formaldehyde (MF)

  • Hard, durable and scratch resistant
  • Resistant to water and heat
  • Cannot be reformed once set
Examples
Kitchen worktopsTablewareLaminates
THERMOSETTING

Polyester Resin (PR)

  • Very water resistant
  • Hard once cured; cannot be reformed
  • Good chemical and heat resistance
  • Brittle on its own — often reinforced with fibreglass (GRP)
Examples
Boat hulls (GRP)Car body panelsAdhesive
THERMOSETTING

Epoxy Resin (ER)

  • Extremely strong adhesive
  • Two-part system — resin and hardener mixed together
  • Excellent resistance to heat and chemicals
  • Cannot be reformed once cured
Examples
Aerospace adhesiveCircuit boardsCoatings
BIOPOLYMER · THERMOPLASTIC

PLA — Polylactic Acid

  • Made from plants (e.g. corn starch) — not from oil
  • Biodegradable — breaks down if buried in soil
  • A biopolymer — unlike conventional plastics
  • Used in 3D printers (a CAD/CAM process)
Does not come from oil
Biodegradable — decomposes naturally
Biopolymer — biological origin
Specification note: WJEC includes Melamine Formaldehyde (MF) for kitchen worktops and tableware, and Epoxy Resin (ER) as a very strong adhesive used in aerospace. Both are thermosetting plastics. Both may appear in exam questions.

Hand Tools

Spec §2.2 — Designing & Making

Measuring Tools

Digital Vernier Caliper

  • Most accurate measuring tool in the workshop
  • Measures to 0.01mm (2 decimal places)
  • Can measure internal and external dimensions
  • Use when material is approximately 2.5mm thick

Steel Rule

  • Accurate to 0.5mm
  • Used for wood and metal
  • Measurements in mm and cm
  • Use when material is approximately 25mm

Tape Measure

  • Accurate to 1mm
  • Used for longer measurements (e.g. a 2.5m board)

Joining Metals

Spec §2.2 — Metals In-depth

Permanent Joining

Soldering (Soft Soldering)

  • Uses a low melting point metal (solder) as filler
  • Electrically heated soldering iron
  • Relatively weak joint
  • Mainly used for electronic components (circuit boards)

Brazing (Hard Soldering)

  • Uses a brass filler rod at approximately 800°C — much hotter than soldering
  • Uses a gas torch
  • Stronger than soldering, weaker than welding
  • Used for bicycle frames — bonds steel with brass filler

Welding

  • Strongest method of joining metals
  • Both pieces are heated, melted and fused together
  • Steel filler rod — becomes one piece of steel
Types
Gas (Oxyacetylene) ~2000°C Arc welding (electric) MIG welding (fed wire) Spot welding (thin sheet)
Arc and MIG welding produce UV light — a full welding mask must be worn to protect the eyes from serious damage.

Temporary / Non-Permanent Joining

Nuts, Bolts & Screws

  • Most common temporary fixing — can be undone with tools
  • Different head types: hexagonal, torx, Allen/socket
  • Spring washers — prevent nuts vibrating loose (e.g. motorbike engines)
  • Nyloc nuts — nylon insert grips bolt thread; used in aircraft
  • Countersinking — creates flush surface finish

Pop Riveting

  • Joins thin sheet metal (and acrylic and wood)
  • Only needs access to one side of the material
  • Technically permanent but can be drilled out
  • Used on aircraft fuselages and sheet metal products
  • Process: drill hole → insert rivet → pull pin until it shears → surfaces pulled together

Thread Cutting (Taps & Dies)

  • Tap — cuts internal thread inside a hole
  • Die — cuts external thread on a rod
  • Turn a couple of turns then back to clear cuttings
  • Used in mild steel and non-ferrous metals

Sustainability

Spec §2 — Six Rs + Materials
MORE SUSTAINABLE

Timber / Wood

  • From managed forestry — trees cut, more planted (2+ for every 1 removed)
  • Trees absorb CO₂ as they grow — helps reduce global warming
  • Biodegradable — will rot away naturally
  • FSC logo = sustainably sourced timber
  • Can also use recycled timber from old buildings and pallets
LESS SUSTAINABLE

Plastic

  • Made from oil — a finite resource
  • Oil formed millions of years ago; cannot be replenished quickly
  • Most plastics are not biodegradable — remain in landfill
  • Recycling is possible but a large proportion still goes to landfill
Sustainability
Meeting today’s needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their needs.
Landfill
Burying rubbish in the ground. Plastic in landfill will not biodegrade.
Biodegradable
Will break down naturally if buried in soil. Wood and PLA are biodegradable; most plastics are not.
Finite resource
A resource that will eventually run out, such as oil or coal. The opposite of renewable.
CO₂ / Greenhouse Gas
Carbon dioxide traps heat in the atmosphere, contributing to global warming. Trees absorb CO₂ as they grow.
FSC Logo
Forestry Stewardship Council. Guarantees timber is from a responsibly managed, sustainable forest.

The Six Rs of Sustainability Spec

R
Reduce
Use less material. Reduce packaging. Consuming less protects finite resources.
R
Rethink
Is there a better way? Did we need the product at all? Could something else do the job with less damage?
R
Refuse
Do not buy products that harm the environment. Say no to unnecessary upgrades or wasteful packaging.
R
Recycle
Convert products back into basic materials to be remade. E.g. tyres into pencil cases; trolleys into chairs.
R
Reuse
Use the product or its parts again. Extends the product’s life. E.g. reusable shopping bags.
R
Repair
Mend before throwing away. Much more sustainable than buying new. E.g. repairing phones or computers.

Worked Exam Question — Glass vs Plastic Bottle

“Discuss the sustainability of a glass bottle and a plastic bottle.”

Glass Bottle

  • ✓Made from sand — a plentiful resource
  • ✓Reusable and fully recyclable
  • ~Takes energy to produce — not entirely green

Plastic Bottle

  • ✗Made from oil — a finite resource
  • ~Can be recycled
  • ✗Takes energy to produce
  • ✗Large proportion ends up in landfill; not biodegradable

Health & Safety

Spec — Safe Working Practices
Key exam tip: Do not confuse general school rules with health and safety rules. Only give rules that physically keep you safe in the workshop.

Safety Signs

Red — Prohibitive
Circle with a line through it. Tells you not to do something. E.g. “Do not enter”, “Do not use this machinery”.
Blue — Mandatory
Tells you that you must do something. E.g. “Wear gloves when using this equipment”, “Safety goggles must be worn”.
Yellow — Warning
Warns you of danger. E.g. “Wet floor”, “High voltage”, “Flammable materials”.
Green — Safe Condition
Indicates safety information such as emergency exits, first aid points, and fire assembly areas.

Safety Equipment

Safety Goggles

  • Protect eyes from flying particles and debris
  • Worn when using machines, chisels, and soldering iron

Ear Defenders

  • Protect hearing from loud machines such as the angle grinder
  • Especially important for long-term hearing protection

Dust Mask

  • Protects lungs from inhaling dust and small particles
  • Used when sanding by hand or with a machine

Leather Gloves

  • Used for handling hot metal
  • Do not wear when using a pillar drill — can get caught and cause injury

Face Shield

  • Covers whole face — used for wood turning
  • Protects from larger flying particles

Welding Mask

  • Protects face and eyes from UV light during arc welding
  • UV can cause severe eye damage

Workshop Safety Rules

Tying Back Long Hair

Hair can catch in rotating machinery such as a pillar drill, or on the soldering iron — a serious hazard.

Wearing an Apron

Protects clothes from paint and glue. Cotton aprons are fire retardant — much safer than nylon clothing.

Clamp Work Securely

When using the pillar drill, always clamp the workpiece — prevents it spinning and causing injury.

Good Ventilation (Soldering)

Soldering fumes are harmful — ensure windows are open or work in a large open space.

COSHH Spec

Control Of Substances Hazardous to Health

A set of legally required regulations that must be followed in all workshops when using substances that may damage health. All hazardous products display warning symbols.

Oil-based paintsFumes can be harmful if inhaled
Glues (e.g. Tensol)Strong solvent-based adhesives
AcidsCorrosive — can cause burns
Sanding dustFine particles damage lungs over time
Solvents / Paint thinnersFlammable and toxic if inhaled
Spray paintFine aerosol particles — respiratory hazard

COSHH Hazard Symbols Include:

ToxicHarmfulFlammableExplosiveDangerous to environmentOxidisingCorrosive
Exam questions on COSHH: “Name three substances covered by COSHH in the workshop” → Tensol glue, spray paint, sanding dust. “Name two precautions when soldering” → Wear goggles and ensure good ventilation. “Name three precautions when using a pillar drill” → Wear goggles, tie back hair, clamp work securely.

Legislation & Regulation

Spec — Legal & Ethical Issues
What the spec requires: You must be able to name relevant legislation, explain what it means for designers and manufacturers, and apply it to given situations. Questions often ask you to “name a law that protects the consumer” or “explain how a designer must consider legislation.”

Consumer Protection

Consumer Rights Act

2015

Replaced earlier Sale of Goods Act and Supply of Goods and Services Act. Sets out what consumers are legally entitled to when they buy products or services.

  • Products must be of satisfactory quality — free from defects and durable
  • Products must be fit for purpose — must do what they are intended to do
  • Products must be as described — must match any description given by the seller
  • Consumers have the right to a repair, replacement or refund if goods are faulty

Trade Descriptions Act

1968

Makes it a criminal offence for a business to apply a false or misleading trade description to goods or services.

  • Descriptions on labels, in adverts, and by sales staff must be accurate
  • Covers claims about material, composition, fitness for purpose, and place of manufacture
  • Designers and manufacturers must ensure product descriptions are truthful

Product Liability (Consumer Protection Act)

1987

Holds manufacturers, importers and suppliers responsible if a defective product causes injury or damage to property.

  • Consumers do not need to prove negligence — just that the product was defective
  • Encourages manufacturers to design and test products thoroughly
  • Applies to physical goods and components within products

Intellectual Property

Copyright

Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988

Protects original creative works such as drawings, photographs, written work, music, and software automatically from the moment they are created.

  • Lasts for the creator’s lifetime plus 70 years
  • Designers cannot copy another designer’s work without permission
  • No registration required; protection is automatic

Patents

Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988

Protects new inventions and how they work. Gives the holder exclusive rights to make, use or sell the invention.

  • Must be registered with the Intellectual Property Office (IPO)
  • Lasts up to 20 years
  • Inventor must disclose how the invention works upon registration
  • Allows the inventor to profit and prevent others copying the invention

Registered Trademarks

Trade Marks Act 1994

Protects brand identity — logos, names, slogans and packaging that distinguish a company’s goods from others.

  • Must be registered; indicated by the ® symbol
  • Lasts 10 years and can be renewed indefinitely
  • Prevents competitors from using the same or very similar branding

Registered Designs

Registered Designs Act 1949 (as amended)

Protects the visual appearance of a product — its shape, pattern, texture, colour or ornamentation.

  • Must be registered with the IPO
  • Lasts up to 25 years (renewed every 5 years)
  • Covers the look of the product, not how it works (that is covered by patents)

Health, Safety & Environment

Health and Safety at Work Act

1974

The primary piece of legislation covering occupational health and safety in the United Kingdom. Applies to all workplaces, including school workshops.

  • Employers must ensure the health, safety and welfare of all employees
  • Employees must take reasonable care of their own safety and that of others
  • Risk assessments must be carried out before using equipment or materials
  • Manufacturers must design and make products that are safe to use

COSHH Regulations

Control of Substances Hazardous to Health 2002

Requires employers and teachers to assess the risks from hazardous substances used in the workplace or workshop and put measures in place to control exposure.

  • Hazardous substances must be identified and assessed before use
  • Appropriate PPE must be provided and used
  • Hazard symbols must be displayed on substances
  • Examples: Tensol glue, spray paint, sanding dust, acids, solvents

Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE)

Regulations 2013

Requires manufacturers and retailers to take responsibility for the safe disposal and recycling of electronic products at end of life.

  • Manufacturers must fund collection, treatment and recycling of old equipment
  • Retailers must accept old products when selling a replacement
  • Encourages designers to consider disassembly and recyclability from the start
  • Examples: mobile phones, computers, fridges, televisions

Restriction of Hazardous Substances (RoHS)

Directive 2002 / Regulations 2012

Restricts the use of specific hazardous materials found in electronic and electrical equipment.

  • Bans or limits substances including lead, mercury, cadmium and certain flame retardants
  • Applies to manufacturers producing electronic products for sale in the UK and EU
  • Reduces toxic materials entering landfill and the environment

Standards & Marking

British Standards (BSI)

Ongoing

The British Standards Institution sets voluntary standards that products must meet to demonstrate safety and quality. Meeting a standard is not always required by law, but it provides assurance to consumers and businesses.

  • Products meeting a standard can display the Kitemark
  • Covers a wide range, from cycle helmets to electrical plugs
  • Designers should aim to meet relevant standards at the specification stage

CE Marking / UKCA Marking

Ongoing (UKCA introduced post-2021)

CE marking indicates a product meets European Union safety, health and environmental requirements. Since Brexit, the UK Conformity Assessed (UKCA) mark has been introduced for the UK market.

  • Required for many product categories before sale in the UK or EU
  • Manufacturers are responsible for ensuring their products comply
  • Covers toys, electrical equipment, personal protective equipment, and more

Applying Legislation in the Exam

Exam questions on legislation typically ask you to name a relevant law and explain how it affects a designer or manufacturer. Use the structure below:

Question type: Consumer protection

Name a law that protects consumers who buy a faulty product.
Answer: The Consumer Rights Act 2015 means products must be of satisfactory quality, fit for purpose and as described. If faulty, the consumer is entitled to a repair, replacement or refund.

Question type: Intellectual property

A designer creates a new product shape. How can they protect it?
Answer: They could register a Registered Design (protects appearance, lasts 25 years) or apply for a patent (protects the invention, lasts 20 years).

Question type: Workshop legislation

Name a law that applies to using hazardous substances in a school workshop.
Answer: COSHH (Control of Substances Hazardous to Health) 2002 requires that hazardous substances are assessed, PPE is used, and hazard symbols are displayed.
